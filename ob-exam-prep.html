I

Organizational Behaviour

Definition · Models · Climate · Structure

2-Mark Questions
2 Marks Define Organizational Behaviour. ▾
Answer

Organizational Behaviour (OB) is the systematic study of human behaviour within organizational settings. It examines how individuals, groups, and structures influence and are influenced by behaviour inside organizations, with the goal of improving organizational effectiveness.

💡 Key phrase to remember: "Study of individual, group, and structure impact on behaviour inside organizations."

2 Marks What is Organizational Climate? ▾
Answer

Organizational Climate refers to the overall psychological atmosphere or "feel" of a workplace. It is the shared perception of employees about the policies, practices, and procedures of their organization.

It is shaped by: leadership style, communication patterns, reward systems, and trust levels among employees.

2 Marks What is the scope of OB? ▾
Answer

The scope of OB covers three levels:

  • Individual Level: Personality, perception, motivation, learning, attitudes.
  • Group Level: Team dynamics, leadership, communication, group decision-making.
  • Organizational Level: Structure, culture, change management, conflict resolution.
2 Marks List the OB models. ▾
Answer

The four OB models are:

  1. Autocratic Model – Based on power and authority
  2. Custodial Model – Based on economic resources and money
  3. Supportive Model – Based on leadership and participation
  4. Collegial Model – Based on partnership and teamwork
5-Mark Questions
5 Marks Explain the need and importance of Organizational Behaviour. ▾
Answer

OB is essential for modern organizations due to the following reasons:

  • 1. Understanding People: Organizations are made of people. OB helps managers understand what motivates employees, how they think, and how they behave — enabling better management decisions.
  • 2. Improving Productivity: By understanding motivation and behaviour, managers can create conditions that maximize employee performance and output.
  • 3. Managing Conflicts: Workplace conflicts are inevitable. OB provides frameworks to identify causes and resolve conflicts constructively.
  • 4. Better Communication: OB studies communication patterns and barriers, helping organizations improve the flow of information at all levels.
  • 5. Managing Change: Organizations must constantly adapt. OB helps leaders understand resistance to change and design effective change management strategies.
  • 6. Developing Leadership: OB identifies effective leadership styles and helps develop leaders who can motivate and guide teams toward organizational goals.
  • 7. Reducing Turnover: Satisfied employees stay longer. OB helps create a positive work culture that retains talent and reduces costly employee turnover.

💡 In exams, always write at least 5 points with a one-line explanation each for 5-mark answers.

10-Mark Questions
10 Marks Define OB. Explain its nature, scope, and importance in detail. ▾
Answer

Definition: Organizational Behaviour (OB) is the systematic study of how people behave individually, in groups, and within organizational structures, and how that behaviour affects organizational performance and effectiveness.

Nature of OB:

  • It is a Science: Uses research, observation, and data to study behaviour patterns.
  • It is an Art: Applying OB knowledge to manage people requires creativity and skill.
  • Interdisciplinary: Draws from Psychology (individual), Sociology (groups), Anthropology (culture), and Economics.
  • Humanistic: Treats employees as whole human beings with needs, emotions, and motivations.
  • Situational: No universal solution — the best approach depends on the context.
  • Goal-Oriented: Ultimately aims to improve organizational effectiveness and employee well-being.

Scope of OB (Three Levels):

  • Individual Level: Covers personality, perception, motivation, learning, attitudes, and emotions.
  • Group Level: Covers team dynamics, communication, leadership, conflict, and group decision-making.
  • Organizational Level: Covers organizational culture, climate, change management, structure, and power politics.

Importance of OB:

  • Helps managers understand and predict employee behaviour.
  • Improves productivity by identifying motivating factors.
  • Provides tools to manage conflict and improve teamwork.
  • Helps leaders develop effective communication strategies.
  • Guides organizations through change smoothly.
  • Creates positive work environments that reduce stress and turnover.
  • Builds strong organizational culture aligned with goals.

💡 Exam Tip: For 10-mark answers, write: Definition → Nature (5–6 points) → Scope (3 levels) → Importance (5–6 points). Always use headings.

II

Individual Behaviour

Personality · Learning · Perception · EI · Attitudes

2-Mark Questions
2 Marks Define Personality. ▾
Answer

Personality is the unique and relatively stable pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviours that characterizes a person over time and across situations. It represents the sum total of ways in which an individual reacts to and interacts with others.

Key factors: Heredity (genes), Environment (family, culture, experiences), and Situation.

2 Marks What is Emotional Intelligence? Name its components. ▾
Answer

Emotional Intelligence (EI) is the ability to recognize, understand, manage, and effectively use emotions — both your own and others' — to guide thinking and behaviour.

5 Components (Goleman): Self-Awareness, Self-Regulation, Motivation, Empathy, Social Skills.

2 Marks Define Perception. What is the Halo Effect? ▾
Answer

Perception is the process by which individuals select, organize, and interpret sensory information to give meaning to their environment. Two people can perceive the same thing differently.

Halo Effect: A perceptual error where one positive trait of a person influences the overall judgment of that person. Example: "She is always well-dressed, so she must be highly competent."

2 Marks What is Learning? Define Operant Conditioning. ▾
Answer

Learning is any relatively permanent change in behaviour that results from experience or practice.

Operant Conditioning (B.F. Skinner): A learning theory stating that behaviour is shaped by its consequences. Behaviour followed by positive reinforcement (reward) increases; behaviour followed by punishment decreases.

5-Mark Questions
5 Marks Explain the Big Five Personality Model (OCEAN). ▾
Answer

The Big Five Model is the most widely accepted personality framework. It identifies five core dimensions:

  • O — Openness to Experience: Degree of curiosity, creativity, and willingness to try new things. High scorers love learning and new experiences. Low scorers prefer routine and familiarity.
  • C — Conscientiousness: Degree of organization, discipline, and dependability. High scorers are goal-oriented and reliable. Strongest predictor of job performance.
  • E — Extraversion: Degree of sociability, assertiveness, and energy. Extraverts are outgoing and talkative. Introverts prefer solitude and reflection.
  • A — Agreeableness: Degree of cooperativeness, kindness, and trust. Agreeable people are easy to work with and good team players.
  • N — Neuroticism: Degree of emotional instability, anxiety, and moodiness. High scorers get stressed easily. Low scorers are emotionally stable.

💡 Remember: OCEAN — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.

5 Marks Explain the theories of Learning in OB. ▾
Answer
  • 1. Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Learning through association. A neutral stimulus is repeatedly paired with an unconditioned stimulus until it alone produces the response. Example: Pavlov's dog salivated at a bell sound. In organizations: employees may feel anxious whenever a certain manager appears.
  • 2. Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Learning through consequences. Four tools: (a) Positive Reinforcement — give reward to increase behaviour (bonus for meeting targets). (b) Negative Reinforcement — remove punishment to increase behaviour. (c) Punishment — unpleasant outcome to decrease behaviour. (d) Extinction — remove reward to eliminate behaviour.
  • 3. Social Learning Theory (Bandura): People learn by observing others. Four processes: Attention (observe role model), Retention (remember what was observed), Reproduction (reproduce the behaviour), Motivation (have reason to reproduce it). Example: New employees learn workplace norms by watching experienced colleagues.
10-Mark Questions
10 Marks Explain Perception in detail — factors, errors, and its importance in OB. ▾
Answer

Definition: Perception is the process by which individuals organize and interpret their sensory impressions in order to give meaning to their environment. Reality is not what IS, but what we PERCEIVE it to be.

Factors Influencing Perception:

  • The Perceiver: Attitudes, motives, interests, experiences, and expectations of the person perceiving.
  • The Target: Novelty, motion, sounds, size, background, proximity of the object being perceived.
  • The Situation: Time, work setting, and social context in which the perception occurs.

Perceptual Errors (Very Important):

  • Halo Effect: One good trait influences overall judgment. "She's punctual, so she must be excellent at everything."
  • Horn Effect: Opposite of halo — one negative trait colours the entire view negatively.
  • Stereotyping: Judging someone based on group they belong to. "All engineers are introverts."
  • Selective Perception: We see only what confirms our existing beliefs, ignoring contradictory information.
  • Projection: Attributing our own characteristics to others. An honest person assumes everyone else is honest.
  • Contrast Effect: Evaluating someone in comparison to another rather than independently. After interviewing an excellent candidate, an average candidate seems worse than they are.

Importance in OB:

  • Managers make hiring and promotion decisions based on perception — errors lead to unfair outcomes.
  • Performance appraisals are affected by perceptual biases.
  • Interpersonal conflicts often arise from misperceptions.
  • Understanding perception helps build fairer, more objective workplaces.

💡 Exam Tip: Always include at least 5 perceptual errors with examples. That's where the marks are!

10 Marks Explain Emotional Intelligence and its role in organizational behaviour. ▾
Answer

Definition: Emotional Intelligence (EI) is the ability to identify, understand, manage, and use emotions effectively — both one's own emotions and those of others — to guide thinking, behaviour, and relationships.

Research by Daniel Goleman shows that EI is a better predictor of workplace success than IQ.

Five Components of EI (Goleman's Model):

  • 1. Self-Awareness: The ability to recognize your own emotions and understand how they affect your thoughts and behaviour. A self-aware manager knows when they are stressed and how it influences their decisions.
  • 2. Self-Regulation: The ability to control impulsive emotions and behaviours. Instead of reacting angrily to criticism, a self-regulated person pauses, reflects, and responds calmly.
  • 3. Motivation: A passion to work for internal reasons — not just money. Highly motivated people set challenging goals, maintain optimism even when they fail, and persist through setbacks.
  • 4. Empathy: Understanding the emotional makeup of others. An empathetic manager notices when a team member is struggling and adjusts expectations or offers support.
  • 5. Social Skills: The ability to manage relationships and build networks. Includes communication, conflict management, influence, and teamwork.

Role of EI in OB:

  • Leaders with high EI build stronger, more trusting teams.
  • EI reduces workplace conflicts through empathy and communication.
  • High EI employees handle stress and pressure better.
  • EI predicts leadership effectiveness, job performance, and career success.
  • Organizations with high-EI cultures have better employee engagement and retention.
III

Group Behaviour

Groups · Teams · Communication · Decision Making

2-Mark Questions
2 Marks Define a Group. What are formal and informal groups? ▾
Answer

A Group is two or more individuals who interact and are interdependent, coming together to achieve particular objectives.

Formal Group: Created by the organization for a specific purpose (e.g., project team, department).

Informal Group: Formed naturally by employees based on friendship or common interests (e.g., lunch group, cricket fans at office).

2 Marks What is Groupthink? ▾
Answer

Groupthink is a phenomenon where the desire for harmony and conformity in a group overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives, leading to poor decision-making.

Signs of Groupthink: illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity. Prevention: assign a "devil's advocate" to challenge consensus.

2 Marks What is the Grapevine in organizations? ▾
Answer

The Grapevine is the informal communication network in an organization. It operates outside official channels and spreads information (and rumours) quickly through social interactions.

Characteristics: Fast, uncontrolled, often inaccurate, carries both factual and distorted information. Managers should monitor it and address rumours promptly.

5-Mark Questions
5 Marks Explain Tuckman's Five Stages of Group Development. ▾
Answer

Bruce Tuckman identified five stages that groups go through before becoming fully effective:

  • 1. Forming: Members are polite and cautious. Everyone is getting to know each other. Goals and roles are unclear. High dependence on the leader.
  • 2. Storming: Conflicts arise over roles, ideas, and direction. Members compete for positions. Most groups struggle at this stage. Leadership is tested.
  • 3. Norming: Conflicts resolve. Group norms are established. Trust develops. Members begin to cooperate and share responsibilities.
  • 4. Performing: The group is fully functional and effective. High energy, high morale, and high productivity. Members work interdependently.
  • 5. Adjourning: The group disbands after completing its task. Members reflect on accomplishments and transitions occur.

💡 Remember: Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning

10-Mark Questions
10 Marks Explain Group Decision Making — advantages, disadvantages, and techniques. ▾
Answer

Group Decision Making is the process of making choices through collective deliberation by multiple members working toward a common goal.

Advantages:

  • More Information: Groups combine knowledge and expertise of multiple members, resulting in better-informed decisions.
  • Diverse Perspectives: Different backgrounds lead to more creative and comprehensive solutions.
  • Greater Acceptance: When employees participate in decisions, they are more likely to accept and implement them.
  • Error Detection: Group members can spot each other's mistakes, improving decision quality.
  • Legitimacy: Group decisions are seen as more legitimate and democratic.

Disadvantages:

  • Time-Consuming: Reaching consensus takes longer than individual decisions.
  • Groupthink: Pressure to conform suppresses individual critical thinking.
  • Group Shift: Groups tend to take more extreme positions than individuals would.
  • Dominant Members: Louder or higher-status members may dominate, silencing valuable input.
  • Responsibility Diffusion: Accountability becomes unclear when everyone decides together.

Techniques to Improve Group Decisions:

  • Brainstorming: Generate as many ideas as possible without criticism. Quantity over quality first, then evaluate.
  • Nominal Group Technique (NGT): Members write ideas independently, then share and vote — reduces domination by vocal members.
  • Delphi Technique: Anonymous questionnaires to experts over multiple rounds until consensus — useful for remote groups.
  • Devil's Advocacy: Assign someone to challenge the group's consensus to prevent groupthink.

Leadership and Power

Styles · Theories · Power Bases · Politics

2-Mark Questions
2 Marks Define Leadership. Differentiate Leader from Manager. ▾
Answer

Leadership is the ability to influence a group toward the achievement of a vision or set of goals. A leader inspires people to follow through motivation and trust.

2 Marks What are the five bases of power? (French & Raven) ▾
Answer
  1. Coercive Power: Based on fear of punishment (threats, firing).
  2. Reward Power: Based on ability to give rewards (bonuses, promotions).
  3. Legitimate Power: Based on formal position or title (CEO, manager).
  4. Expert Power: Based on special knowledge or skills.
  5. Referent Power: Based on admiration and personal charisma.
5-Mark Questions
5 Marks Explain the different Leadership Styles. ▾
Answer
  • 1. Autocratic Style: Leader makes all decisions alone without consulting the team. Best for emergencies and unskilled workers. Disadvantage: kills creativity and morale.
  • 2. Democratic Style: Leader involves team in decision-making. Boosts morale, creativity, and commitment. Best for skilled, motivated teams.
  • 3. Laissez-Faire Style: Leader gives full freedom to employees. Best for highly expert, self-motivated individuals like researchers. Risk: chaos if team lacks discipline.
  • 4. Transformational Style: Leader inspires change through a compelling vision. Motivates followers to exceed self-interest for the organization's good. E.g., Steve Jobs.
  • 5. Transactional Style: Based on exchange — rewards for performance. Maintains standards through clear expectations and consequences.
  • 6. Servant Leadership: Leader serves the team first — removes obstacles, provides resources, empowers employees.
10-Mark Questions
10 Marks Explain the Theories of Leadership in detail. ▾
Answer

1. Trait Theory:
Believes leaders are born with certain qualities — intelligence, confidence, integrity, determination, sociability. This theory says leadership is innate. Limitation: Many successful leaders do not possess all these traits.

2. Behavioural Theories:
Shifts focus from WHO the leader is to WHAT the leader does. Two dimensions: Task Orientation (focus on work, deadlines, productivity) and People Orientation (focus on relationships, morale, well-being). The best leaders balance both.
Ohio State Studies: Identified Initiating Structure (task) and Consideration (people).
Michigan Studies: Identified Production-Centred and Employee-Centred styles.
Managerial Grid (Blake & Mouton): 9×9 grid showing concern for production vs concern for people. Ideal: 9,9 — Team Management style.

3. Contingency Theories:
No single best leadership style. The right style depends on the situation.

  • Fiedler's Contingency Model: Match leader to situation. Task-oriented leaders best in very favourable or unfavourable situations. Relationship-oriented leaders best in moderate situations.
  • Situational Leadership (Hersey & Blanchard): Adjust style based on follower's readiness. Four styles: Telling (low readiness) → Selling → Participating → Delegating (high readiness).
  • Path-Goal Theory (House): Leaders motivate by clarifying the path to goals and removing obstacles. Four behaviours: Directive, Supportive, Participative, Achievement-Oriented.
  • Leader-Member Exchange (LMX) Theory: Leaders form different quality relationships with different followers — in-group (favoured) and out-group. High LMX relationships yield better outcomes.

💡 Exam Tip: Always name the theorist (e.g., Fiedler, Hersey & Blanchard). Examiners give marks for correct attribution.

V

Dynamics of OB

Culture · Change · Stress · Job Satisfaction

2-Mark Questions
2 Marks Define Organizational Culture. ▾
Answer

Organizational Culture is the system of shared values, beliefs, norms, and assumptions that guide employee behaviour and distinguish one organization from another. It is often described as "how we do things around here."

Strong cultures have widely shared, deeply held core values. Weak cultures lack clarity and consistency in values.

2 Marks What is Work Stress? Name any four stressors. ▾
Answer

Work Stress is the harmful physical and emotional response that occurs when job demands exceed the employee's ability to cope, threatening their well-being.

Four Stressors:

  1. Task Demands — overload, tight deadlines
  2. Role Conflict — conflicting expectations
  3. Interpersonal Demands — difficult coworkers, bullying
  4. Work-Life Imbalance — excessive working hours
2 Marks What is Resistance to Change? Give two reasons. ▾
Answer

Resistance to Change is the refusal of individuals or groups to accept, adopt, or adjust to a change in their work environment, processes, or conditions.

Two key reasons:

  • Fear of the Unknown: People are uncomfortable with uncertainty about what the change will bring.
  • Habit: Established routines are comfortable. Change disrupts familiar patterns.
5-Mark Questions
5 Marks Explain Lewin's Three-Step Change Model. ▾
Answer

Kurt Lewin's model provides a simple, effective framework for implementing organizational change:

  • Step 1 — Unfreeze: This is the preparation stage. The organization must break down existing mindsets, habits, and processes. Employees must understand WHY change is necessary. This involves creating a sense of urgency, communicating clearly, and addressing fears. Without proper unfreezing, people will resist change.
  • Step 2 — Change (Move/Transition): The actual change is implemented. New processes, behaviours, or systems are introduced. This is the most uncertain and difficult stage — people need support, training, clear communication, and time to adjust.
  • Step 3 — Refreeze: The new state is stabilized and made permanent. The organization reinforces the new behaviours through rewards, new policies, and cultural integration. Without refreezing, people often drift back to old habits.

💡 Simple analogy: Think of changing the shape of an ice cube — melt it (unfreeze), reshape it (change), then freeze it again (refreeze).

10-Mark Questions
10 Marks Explain Work Stress — causes, effects, and management strategies in detail. ▾
Answer

Definition: Work Stress is a dynamic condition in which an individual faces demands, constraints, or opportunities that are both uncertain and important, overwhelming their coping capacity.

Types: Eustress (positive stress — challenge, excitement, improves performance) and Distress (negative stress — overwhelms, harms health).

Sources / Causes of Work Stress:

  • Task Demands: Workload overload, unrealistic deadlines, monotonous tasks.
  • Role Demands: Role ambiguity (unclear expectations), role conflict (conflicting demands from multiple supervisors).
  • Interpersonal Demands: Difficult relationships with colleagues or managers, workplace bullying, lack of social support.
  • Organizational Structure: Excessive rules, rigid hierarchy, no employee autonomy or participation.
  • Environmental Factors: Poor working conditions, noise, overcrowding.
  • Work-Life Conflict: Long hours leaving insufficient time for personal and family life.

Effects of Stress:

  • Physiological: Headaches, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, weakened immunity, sleep disorders.
  • Psychological: Anxiety, depression, emotional exhaustion, burnout, reduced job satisfaction.
  • Behavioural: Absenteeism, low productivity, aggression, poor decision-making, substance abuse.

Individual Stress Management:

  • Time management — prioritize tasks, set realistic goals.
  • Regular physical exercise — reduces cortisol and improves mood.
  • Relaxation techniques — meditation, deep breathing, yoga, mindfulness.
  • Social support — talking to family, friends, or counsellors.
  • Cognitive reframing — changing perspective on stressful events.

Organizational Stress Management:

  • Improved selection and placement — matching people to suitable roles.
  • Goal-setting programs — clear, achievable objectives reduce uncertainty.
  • Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) — confidential counselling services.
  • Flexible work arrangements — remote work, flextime, compressed weeks.
  • Wellness programs — gym memberships, mental health days, healthy food options.
  • Participative management — involving employees in decisions reduces powerlessness.

💡 Exam Tip: Divide your answer into: Definition → Types → Causes (6 points) → Effects (3 types) → Individual Management → Organizational Management. That's a full 10-mark answer.

10 Marks Explain Organizational Change — types, process, and how to overcome resistance. ▾
Answer

Definition: Organizational change is the process of transitioning from a current state to a desired future state to increase organizational effectiveness in response to internal or external forces.

Types of Change:

  • Planned vs Unplanned: Planned change is deliberate (e.g., ERP system implementation). Unplanned is reactive (e.g., COVID-19 forcing remote work).
  • Incremental vs Radical: Incremental is gradual improvement. Radical (transformational) is a complete overhaul of structure, strategy, or culture.
  • Proactive vs Reactive: Proactive change anticipates future needs. Reactive responds to current problems.

The Change Process (Lewin's Model): Unfreeze → Change → Refreeze (explained separately).

Individual Sources of Resistance:

  • Habit, Fear of unknown, Economic concerns, Selective information processing, Security threat.

Organizational Sources of Resistance:

  • Structural inertia, Limited focus, Group norms, Threat to power, Resource limitations.

Overcoming Resistance — Kotter's Strategies:

  • Education & Communication: Explain the logic and need for change before it happens.
  • Participation & Involvement: Allow those who resist to participate in designing the change.
  • Facilitation & Support: Offer counselling, training, and emotional support during transition.
  • Negotiation & Agreement: Offer incentives to key resistors in exchange for cooperation.
  • Manipulation: Selectively share information to gain compliance (use carefully — can backfire).
  • Explicit/Implicit Coercion: Force compliance through threats. Last resort — damages trust.
